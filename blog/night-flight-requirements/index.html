Night flight requirements for the PPL

The night portion of FAR 61.109 has three separate parts that students routinely miss. Here is each one in plain English.

Published May 29, 2026 · SoloTrack Team

Home›Blog›Night flight requirements
Disclaimer: This is a plain-English summary of FAR 61.109(a)(2)(ii) for an airplane single-engine land private pilot certificate, not legal or aeronautical advice. Always read the current Federal Aviation Regulations and confirm the specifics with your CFI.

Most of FAR 61.109 is measured in hours. The night requirement is different: it is a set of three discrete tasks, and you need all three. Because they hide inside one subparagraph, students often arrive at checkride prep thinking they are "done with night" when they are missing the towered-airport landings or the 100 NM cross-country.

The three night requirements

1. Three hours of night dual

You need at least 3 hours of night flight training with an authorized instructor in a single-engine airplane. This is the umbrella time requirement, and the two tasks below happen inside it.

2. One night cross-country over 100 NM total

You need one dual night cross-country flight of more than 100 nautical miles total distance. "Total distance" is the sum of the legs, not a straight-line measurement — and it must be flown at night with your instructor.

3. Ten night takeoffs and landings at a towered airport

You need 10 takeoffs and 10 landings to a full stop, at an airport with an operating control tower, with each landing involving a traffic-pattern flight. The two details that catch people out:

  • Full stop — touch-and-goes do not count here.
  • Operating control tower — the tower has to be operating at the time, which can mean planning around tower hours at smaller fields.

What counts as "night"?

This is a common source of confusion because the FAA uses different night definitions for different purposes. For the 61.109 training requirements, the relevant definition involves the period of night as defined in the regulations; for logging and for 61.57 night currency, the "one hour after sunset to one hour before sunrise" window applies to full-stop landings. When in doubt, fly well after dark and confirm the specifics with your CFI.

Plan it as one or two flights

Many students knock out the night requirement efficiently by combining tasks: fly the 100+ NM dual night cross-country to a towered field, do several full-stop pattern laps there, and continue building toward the 10 landings. A little planning around tower hours and a towered destination saves you a second dedicated night.

Track each night sub-requirement separately

Because these are separate tasks, a single "night hours" number is not enough — you have to know how many towered full-stop night landings you actually have and whether the 100 NM cross-country is logged.
