FAR 61.57 currency explained

Day, night, and instrument currency — what each one requires, what counts, and how to keep yourself legal to carry passengers.

Published May 29, 2026 · SoloTrack Team

Home›Blog›FAR 61.57 currency explained
Disclaimer: This is a plain-English explanation of how SoloTrack thinks about FAR 61.57, not legal or aeronautical advice. Always read the current Federal Aviation Regulations and confirm the specifics with your CFI before relying on them.

Currency is one of the most misunderstood ideas in early training. Students confuse it with the FAR 61.109 experience they need to earn the certificate, but they are two different systems. FAR 61.109 is about earning the private pilot certificate. FAR 61.57 is about staying recent enough to carry passengers (and, for instrument, to fly under IFR) after you already hold it. You can be a certificated private pilot and still not be legal to take a friend flying today.

FAR 61.57 has three independent parts. You can be current in one and not the others.

1. Day currency — FAR 61.57(a)

To carry passengers during the day, you must have made 3 takeoffs and 3 landings within the preceding 90 days, acting as the sole manipulator of the controls, in an aircraft of the same category, class, and (if a type rating applies) type.

  • Category/class matter. Landings in an airplane single-engine land keep you current for airplane single-engine land — not for a multiengine or a different class.
  • The landings do not need to be full stop for day currency in most airplanes (tailwheel airplanes are the well-known exception).
  • It is a rolling 90-day window. Your currency expires when your third-most-recent landing slides past 90 days ago.

This rule is about carrying passengers. You can always fly solo to regain currency.

2. Night currency — FAR 61.57(b)

To carry passengers at night, you need 3 takeoffs and 3 landings to a full stop within the preceding 90 days, during the period from one hour after sunset to one hour before sunrise.

  • Full stop is required — touch-and-goes do not count for night currency.
  • The night window (one hour after sunset to one hour before sunrise) is narrower than "civil twilight" definitions used elsewhere, which trips people up.
  • Night currency is separate from day currency. Being night current also satisfies day currency, but not the other way around.

3. Instrument currency — FAR 61.57(c)

To act as pilot in command under IFR or in actual instrument conditions, you must have performed, within the preceding 6 calendar months, the following tasks: six instrument approaches, holding procedures and tasks, and intercepting and tracking courses using navigational electronic systems.

  • "Calendar months" is the key phrase. Experience counts through the end of the month. Approaches flown in March keep that task current through the end of September.
  • It is not just approaches — holding and intercepting/tracking are required too.
  • If you let it lapse, there is a 6-calendar-month grace period in which you can regain currency by logging the tasks (often under a safety pilot or in an approved simulator). After that grace period, you need an instrument proficiency check (IPC) with an authorized instructor or examiner.

Currency vs. proficiency

Meeting 61.57 makes you legal. It does not necessarily make you proficient. Three quick pattern laps after a long layoff satisfy the regulation but may not rebuild your sight picture. Treat currency as a floor, and use your own honest judgment — and your CFI's — about whether you are ready to take passengers.
