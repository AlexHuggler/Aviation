Checkride preparation: what your DPE expects

The experience requirements, the endorsements, and the one thing students underestimate — a logbook a Designated Pilot Examiner can actually review.

Published May 29, 2026 · SoloTrack Team

Home›Blog›Checkride preparation guide
Disclaimer: This is general preparation guidance, not legal or aeronautical advice and not a list of everything any individual checkride requires. Your CFI and your examiner determine readiness. Always confirm requirements with your CFI and the current Federal Aviation Regulations.

A checkride is two events: a verification that you are eligible, and a practical test of whether you are proficient. Students pour energy into the second and underinvest in the first — then lose the morning of the checkride to a logbook the examiner cannot reconcile. Here is how to arrive ready on both fronts.

1. Meet the experience requirements — and be able to show them

Your examiner needs to see that you meet the FAR 61.109 aeronautical-experience requirements: 40 hours total, 20 dual, 10 solo, 5 solo cross-country, the night requirements, and the instrument training, plus the solo long cross-country and recent test-prep items. The trap is the discrete items — the night towered landings, the 150 NM solo cross-country, the checkride-prep flight within the preceding two calendar months. Run the PPL progress checker and the checkride readiness checklist a couple of weeks out so there are no surprises.

2. Confirm eligibility paperwork

Beyond flight experience, you will generally need a current medical and student pilot certificate, a passed knowledge (written) test, and the required instructor endorsements. Your CFI manages these — confirm with them well before the date which endorsements your specific checkride requires.

3. Make your logbook reviewable

This is where SoloTrack earns its keep. Examiners are reconciling your totals against the requirements; anything that makes that faster helps the day go smoothly:

  • Totals that add up. Categories should be tagged consistently so your dual, solo, cross-country, night, and instrument columns reconcile.
  • Endorsements that are legible and findable. SoloTrack captures CFI endorsements with a PencilKit signature and the CFI's certificate number, and locks the endorsed record to preserve integrity.
  • A clean export. Hand over a printable PDF, or a 22-column CSV, instead of a coffee-stained paper book — see importing your logbook if you are still consolidating from another tool.

4. Currency, not just experience

Make sure you are also current under FAR 61.57 for the flight to the checkride and any required maneuvers. Use the currency calculator if you are unsure.
